# Reality Threshold

A reality threshold is the proposed cutoff a perceptual or imagined signal must cross before the brain treats it as real. The concept comes from Dijkstra and Fleming's work on visual imagery and perception.

The core idea is simple but powerful:

- Perception and imagination can produce overlapping neural and subjective signals.
- Imagined signals are usually weaker than externally driven perceptual signals.
- When the combined subjective signal is vivid or strong enough, the brain may judge it as real.
- Reality monitoring may therefore depend partly on signal strength, not only on knowing whether one intended to imagine something.

This matters because it gives a concrete model for a messy boundary: imagined images, dreams, hallucinations, virtual reality, intrusive imagery, and external perception may differ by degree and classification, not by totally separate mental machinery.

## Not A Claim That Everything Is Arbitrary

Reality threshold does not mean the external world is fake or that all experiences are equally real. It means the felt realness of an experience is constructed by the brain and can sometimes be misclassified.
