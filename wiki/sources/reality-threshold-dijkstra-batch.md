# Reality Threshold - Dijkstra Source Batch

- Topic: how the brain distinguishes perceived visual input from imagined visual imagery

## Primary-Source Claims

Dijkstra and Fleming's 2023 Nature Communications paper proposes that perception and imagination can contribute to a shared subjective signal. Reality judgments depend on whether that mixed signal is strong enough to pass a reality threshold. In the study design, participants imagined oriented lines while viewing noise; on a critical trial, faint real lines could be added. More vivid subjective experiences were more likely to be judged as real, even when the source was internally generated.

The 2025 Neuron follow-up, as reported by UCL and Nature News, adds candidate brain mechanisms. Fusiform gyrus activity tracks a vividness or "reality signal," while anterior insula activity increases when participants judge an image as real. This suggests a possible circuit where visual vividness is evaluated and then classified as real or imagined, though the mechanism remains early-stage and needs further replication.

## Commentary Framing

Quanta frames the work as a modern answer to an old philosophical and psychological question: why do imagined images not normally feel real? It connects the study to the Perky effect, hallucination research, dreaming, virtual reality, and the broader idea that perception is actively constructed.

Nature News emphasizes the 2025 brain-imaging result: the study identifies brain regions associated with distinguishing imagined images from external visual input, but it also warns that self-report tasks can be affected by attention and other cognitive factors.

## Useful Synthesis

Reality threshold is a strong concept for this wiki because it bridges neuroscience, perception, hallucination, imagination, dreaming, VR, and philosophical questions about constructed reality. It is also a useful caution: "realness" is not simply a direct readout of the external world. It is a judgment made by a brain evaluating signal strength, context, and internal/external source cues.

## Open Questions

- How stable is an individual's reality threshold across contexts such as sleep, stress, psychosis, meditation, or drug states?
- Is the reality threshold primarily a visual mechanism, or does a similar threshold operate across auditory, bodily, and social perception?
- How should this model connect to predictive processing, active inference, and interoception?
- Does modern media exposure lower the threshold for treating representations as reality, as the Quanta article speculates, or is that only an interpretive possibility?
